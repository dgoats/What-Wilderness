wilderness

9 conversations
on ecology, abstraction, and the anthropocene

 

The clay comes from the land–

The work has a very close spiritual and personal relationship to the land.

but it is so different from being out here.

I like that the material I'm using records movement, it records the body, and in a very nontoxic way.

There is nothing to fear when I'm working with the material

This is not the experience I've had on the water.

 

One begins to realize that to be pro-choice can also be an ecological position – that we need less humans.

but I still have yet to meet someone who says I'm pro-choice because of this.

Except for my sister who is an ecologist and studies invasive species.

She lives on the Channel Islands and is the only person I've met who says this.

It becomes very uncomfortable, to hear her evoke the death of humans.

I had biophilia. I just did everything that I thought was nature.

I pretended to be a horse all the time

or a cow or a cat or a mouse, I was never human.

and if you look at my early artwork, there are no people in it,

anywhere, there are no humans. and I just wanted there not to be humans.

I like interacting with people, but my art practice was not about that at all.

There's ecophobia.

We're raising a generation of children who are scared of the outdoors

or are so disengaged from it that they don't know how to be in the wilderness.

There are so many niches in terms of what the ecosystem can allow

There are moments here when I forget I'm in New York.

These organisms are so well developed, they have coevolved with us,

so they know how to exploit the bits and pieces we leave.

Is this what would be considered a novel ecosystem?

By the definition, of a site that can't be returned to what it was, that has gone too far in change.

I have a problem with how do we define the baseline
what do we return it to?
